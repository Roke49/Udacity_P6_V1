Summer Heat Waves In The US:
Three Decades of Data

As the earth continues to warm, what effects are we likely to see at the extremes of temperature? If the rise in the Earth's average temperature causes a comparable increase in our typical daily temperature range, the effect would be fairly modest. On the other hand, if we see more prolonged spells of abnormally high temperature - heat waves - there can be profound effects on society and human health: deaths from hyperthermia, severe crop failures, power outages from excessive air conditioning use, etc.

In 2015 the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (“CDC”) released an analysis of what they called “heat wave days” in the U.S. for the years 1980-2010. Heat wave days were defined as 2 or more days in succession when the maximum air temperature (or a calculated heat stress index) exceeded the 95th percentile for that date in that county. What these data show is that there has already been a major increase in the frequency of abnormally hot summer days in the US during an interval in which the increase in global mean temperature is quite modest. A secondary message is that climate changes on a short scale can make it difficult to see the longer term trends, and that the effects of climate change differ significantly across geographic regions.
