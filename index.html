Climate Change And Extreme Heat Events In The US

Understanding the potential impacts of climate change on human health is of key importance as the Earth continues to warm. One area of major concern is how much more morbidity and mortality we may see from severe heat stress. If daily maximum temperatures simply increase by the amount that the Earth’s average temperature is rising, this impact would be relatively small. On the other hand, if a warming climate brings many more severe heat waves, the risk for serious adverse impacts to human health is much higher.

In 2015 the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (“CDC”) released an analysis of what they called “heat wave days” in the U.S. for the years 1980-2010. The data from this analysis are the subject of the visualization that follows. The underlying data are the daily maximum and minimum air temperature, and a calculated heat stress index, for each county in the US for the months of May through September. The definition of heat wave days was 2 or more days in succession when the maximum air temperature (or heat stress index) exceeded the 95th percentile for that date in that county. This visualization depicts the data based on maximum daily temperature. Additionally, data showing the change in global mean surface temperature of the Earth are portrayed
